Context

How do you test a payment system that behaves differently in every country?

TerraPay specialises in international payments — transactions that cross borders, currencies, and regulations. Their internal teams needed a way to run sample payments through the system before anything went live. The challenge: what works in one corridor (say, Brazil to Ghana) doesn't behave the same way in another. A single rigid testing flow wouldn't scale. This project was built in two phases. Phase 1 solved the internal tooling problem. Phase 2 validated the experience with real users in real markets.

Phase 1 — Backend

Challenge 01

How do you test something that behaves differently everywhere?

Cross-border payments aren't one flow. They change by country, currency, and regulation. A payment that succeeds in one corridor will get stuck in another — for completely different reasons. The testing platform had to be flexible enough to handle all of this without requiring a redesign per market.

The key insight: don't design a single testing flow. Design a system that generates flows. Reusable components with dynamic configuration per payment corridor.

What made this hard

  • Each payment corridor had different rules, failure modes, and edge cases
  • Internal teams needed to configure tests without engineering support
  • The design had to work for corridors that didn't exist yet

How I approached it

  • Built a modular system — forms, rules, and projects that combine dynamically
  • Designed reusable constraint handling: date, name, time, payment limits
  • Covered all test outcome states: payment through, payment stuck, failure modes

I moved from designing flows to designing systems that generate flows. The difference matters most when the edge cases are the product.

Phase 1 → Phase 2

Challenge 02

Designing across two layers — internal tooling vs real user experience

Phase 1 was internal tooling for TerraPay employees who understood the payment system deeply. Phase 2 was a completely different problem: a frontend for beta-testers who were new to the platform entirely. The same underlying logic had to power two radically different user experiences.

The tension wasn't just complexity — it was audience. Phase 1 users tolerated technical language and density. Phase 2 users needed simplicity and clarity. The system had to speak both languages.

What made this hard

  • Phase 1 and Phase 2 users had completely different mental models of the product
  • Simplifying the frontend without breaking the backend logic was a constant constraint
  • The design system was owned by a different team — alignment required ongoing coordination

How I navigated it

  • Mapped the shared logic layer — what both phases had in common underneath
  • Designed Phase 2 as a translation layer, not a separate product
  • Kept the design consistent with the external design system while adapting vocabulary for each audience

Designing across layers taught me that the same system can need very different surfaces. The skill is knowing which layer you're designing for — and not letting them bleed into each other.

Phase 2 — Research

Challenge 03

Cross-cultural research — empathising with users from a country you don't share

Phase 2 users were beta-testers based in South America and Africa — people who moonlight on platforms like betatesting.com as a side income, so they're experienced evaluators. But they bring different assumptions, trust signals, and payment habits than users in India. Good research required building genuine rapport before useful answers came out.

"Most users turned out to be genuinely intrigued by the project and wanted to be involved."
Cross-cultural research isn't just about translation. It's about earning the comfort needed for honest feedback. The first 10 minutes of each session were entirely about putting participants at ease — not about the product.

What made this hard

  • Users' assumptions about payment flows differed significantly by region
  • Building participant comfort across cultural distance took deliberate effort
  • The research plan had to be pressure-tested before going live — mock interviews first

How I built the research

  • Built the research plan and interview questionnaire from scratch
  • Ran mock interviews to validate the questionnaire against project goals
  • Adapted approach per participant to account for cultural context
  • Analysed patterns across interviews and translated findings into design decisions
